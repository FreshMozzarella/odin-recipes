Overnight Pizza

Description

Home-made thin-crust dough that uses long fermentation that develops great flavor the longer you wait. Making these dough balls says before allows you to time up baking them the next day at any time. I recommend you buy fresh ingredients as well. Check out the Pizza Sauce Recipe. It is a game-changer and adds a whole new depth of flavor compared to store-bought sauce

A baking stone is not required but gives the pizza a more even baking. This dough recipe does not use a mixer. I feel this gives one a better opportunity to feel and evaluate when the dough is ready. Having a digital thermometer allows us to more accurately measure the rate of fermentation of the dough. Having a plastic scraper helps us manipulate the dough as well

We use instant dry yeast instead of active dry yeast-it gives more efficient rise and has more yeast per weight

See here for more information about baker's percentage and how to scale recipes

Yield: Around five 350 gram dough balls, each of which yield a thin-crust pizza pizza-stone about 12 inches in diameter or a thick-crust iron-skillet pizza.
Bulk fermentation: About 12 hours or more
Proof time: At least 6 hours

Ingredients

Dough

Ingredient Quantity (metric) Quantity (imperial) Baker's Percentage
White Flour 1,000 g 7 ¾ cups 100%
Water 700 g, 90°F to 95°F (32°C to 35°C) 3 cups 70%
Fine sea salt 20 g 1 tbsp + ¾ tsp 2%
Instant dried yeast 0.8 g Scant ¼ tsp 0.08%

Additional Ingredients and Toppings

  • One 350 gram dough ball
  • 3 ounces pizza sauce (see pizza sauce recipe for more info)
  • 4 ounces fresh whole-milk mozzarella cheese, cut into ½ inch thick at most
  • 6-8 whole basil leaves
  • Extra-virgin olive oil for drizzling(optional)
  • Fine-flake sea salt(optional)
  • Chile flakes(optional)

Steps

  1. Hydrate the yeast-Measure 700 grams of water at 90°F to 95°F (32°C to 35°C) into a container. Put 0.8 gram of yeast in a separate, small container. Add about 3 tablespoons of the measured water to the yeast and set aside
  2. Autolyse-Combine the 1,000 grams of flour with the remaining 700 grams of warm water in a 12-quart round tub(most large containers would suffice).Mix by hand just until incorporated. Cover and let rest for 20 to 30 minutes
  3. Mix Sprinkle the 20 grams of salt over the top of the dough.Use a small piece of the autolysed mixture to wipe the remaining yeast from its container, then throw back into tub. Mix by hand, wetting your working hand before mixing the dough doesn't stick to you.(it's fine to re-apply water to hand)Reach underneath the dough and grab a small section. Gently stretch and fold it over the top to the other side of the dough. Repeat three more times with the remaining dough, until the salt and yeast are fully enclosed. Using a pincer-like motion, cut the dough with your thumb and index finger while alternating with folding the dough to fully integrate ingredients. Cut, fold, cut fold, etc. Your target dough temperature should be around 77°F to 78°F (25°C to 26 °)
  4. Fold This dough needs folding ( visual aid on stretching and foldinggently grabbing a corner of the dough, stretching it, and then folding it over the opposite corner as mentioned in previous step) about one or two times. Apply folds 30 to 60 minutes after mixing, giving about 10 to 20 minute breaks in between folds to let the gluten relax. Lightly coat the dough and the bottom of the tub with olive oil to prevent sticking between folds as well. When the dough is 2 to 3 times its original volume (around 12 hours after mixing, it is ready to be divided)
  5. Divide Moderately flour a work surface about 2 feet wide. With floured hands, gently ease the dough out of the tub. With your hands still floured, pick up the dough and ease it back down onto the work surface in a somewhat even shape. Dust the entire top of the dough with some flour, then cut into 5 equal-size pieces with a scraper. Each piece should weigh about 340 grams; or you can eyeball it
  6. Shape the dough into balls Shape each piece of dough into a medium-tight round, working gently and being careful to not to degas the dough too much(visual aid on techniques)
  7. Refrigerate Put the dough balls on a lightly floured baking sheet, leaving space between them to allow for expansion. Lightly oil or flour the tops, cover with plastic wrap, and refrigerate for at least 6 hours, up to 5 days
  8. Preheat the pizza stone About an hour before throwing the pizza in the oven, put your pizza stone or baking sheet on a rack in the upper portion of your oven so the surface is about 8 inches below the broiler. Preheat the oven to 600°F (316°C) or preheat to the highest setting you have on the oven, most ovens don't go over 550°F. Once the oven has preheated, continue heating for another 30 to 45 minutes(less time needed if using a metal baking sheet)
  9. Set up your epic pizza assembly station Give yourself about 2 feet of width on the counter-top. Generously flour the work surface. Position your peel(a cutting board or stiff piece of cardboard also works well as a peel) next to the floured area and dust it with flour. Have sauce, cheese, and basil prepared and at hand, using a ladle or large spoon for the sauce
  10. Shape your pizza Remove the dough ball from the refrigerator, put it on the floured work surface, and gently pat it down a bit to coat the bottom with flour. Leaving about 1 inch of the outer rim undeflated, punch down the middle, then flip the dough over and repeat. Using both hands, grab the rim and lift so the dough hangs down vertically. Let gravity pull the rest of the dough down and stretch it. Run the rim between your hands, working all the way around the circumference of the dough several times. Next, make two fists and position them just inside the rim, with the dough still hanging vertically. Gently stretch and turn the dough repeatedly, still letting the bottom of the dough pull down, expanding the surface. Keep a close eye on the thickness of the dough. You want it thin, but you don't want it to tear or develop holes. Don't panic if this happens-patch it over with dough. Spread the dough on the floured(or use semolina flour) peel and run your hands around the perimeter to shape it into a round and work out the kinks
  11. Superheat the pizza stone After 30 minutes oven has reached its set temperature, you can switch to broil mode for about 5-10 minutes to get the stone/metal very hot to emulate the heat of a traditional pizza oven
  12. Top the pizza Spread with tomato sauce over the dough to within an inch of the edge, smoothing and spreading it with the back of the ladle. Distribute the mozzarella and basil leaves evenly around the pie
  13. Bake Turn the oven setting back to bake. With the peel, gently slide the pizza onto the pizza stone/sheet. Bake for 5 minutes, then switch to the broil setting and broil for 2 minutes, keeping a close eye on the pizza. Bake until the cheese is completely melted and the crust is golden with spots of brown and a few spots of char. If the oil separates out of the cheese, it's overbaked. Use tongs or a fork to slide the pizza from the pizza stone/sheet onto a large plate
  14. Slice and serve Transfer to a large wooden cutting board. Drizzle extra-virgin olive oil lightly over the top if you like, slice, and serve immediately, passing salt or flakes of your choosing over the table. Optimal time to eat this kind of pizza is when the cheese has been melted, but not to wait too long or it will solidify. Enjoy!
